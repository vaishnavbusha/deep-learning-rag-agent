# Sequence-to-Sequence Models

## Encoder-Decoder Framing
Sequence-to-sequence models are designed for problems where both the input and the output are sequences, potentially with different lengths. The core idea is to use an encoder to read the input sequence into an internal representation and a decoder to generate the output sequence one step at a time. This architecture became especially influential in machine translation, where a source sentence in one language must be transformed into a target sentence in another. A strong interview explanation should highlight that Seq2Seq changed how people thought about variable-length transduction tasks. Instead of hand-engineering task-specific pipelines, the model learned a mapping from one sequence domain to another. The encoder-decoder framing also makes it easier to reason about modularity, because encoding and generation are separate responsibilities even though they are trained jointly.

## Why Context Bottlenecks Matter
Early Seq2Seq models often compressed the entire input sequence into a single fixed-length context vector before decoding. This worked reasonably well for short sequences, but performance degraded on longer inputs because too much information had to pass through a narrow bottleneck. In practical terms, the decoder might lose fine-grained details from earlier parts of the source sequence, which hurts translation quality and long-range reasoning. This limitation is useful in interviews because it motivates later improvements such as attention. A strong answer should not just say there was a bottleneck, but explain why it mattered: information capacity and access were constrained at exactly the point where the decoder needed flexible access to source content. This bottleneck became one of the most important arguments for moving toward more expressive alignment mechanisms.

## How Seq2Seq Connects to Later Architectures
Seq2Seq is historically important because it established a general recipe for structured generation from sequential inputs, and many later architectures preserved that pattern even after replacing the recurrent backbone. Attention-enhanced encoder-decoder models and transformers still follow the same broad idea: encode the source, then decode a target conditioned on that encoded information. In interview settings, Seq2Seq is valuable as a bridge topic because it connects RNNs, LSTMs, attention, and modern language models. A solid explanation should also note that Seq2Seq models are evaluated not only by whether they generate fluent outputs, but by whether those outputs are aligned with the source content. That requirement makes retrieval grounding, context preservation, and source conditioning conceptually relevant to this assignment’s RAG system as well.
