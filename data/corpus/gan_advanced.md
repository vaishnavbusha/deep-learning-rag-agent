# Generative Adversarial Networks

## Adversarial Training Setup
Generative Adversarial Networks consist of two models trained in opposition: a generator that produces synthetic samples and a discriminator that attempts to distinguish real data from generated data. The generator improves by learning to fool the discriminator, while the discriminator improves by becoming better at detecting synthetic outputs. This setup is often described as a minimax game because each model’s objective depends on the other model’s behavior. A strong technical explanation should emphasize that GAN training is not supervised in the ordinary sense. The generator never receives ground-truth target images for its outputs. Instead, it receives learning signals indirectly through the discriminator’s judgments. That makes GANs conceptually elegant and practically difficult, because both networks are changing simultaneously during optimization.

## Why GANs Are Powerful but Unstable
GANs became important because they can produce highly realistic samples, often much sharper than outputs from simpler likelihood-based generative models. Their strength comes from the adversarial objective, which pressures the generator to match the distributional characteristics of real data rather than merely minimizing a pointwise reconstruction loss. However, that same training setup is unstable. If the discriminator becomes too strong, the generator may receive weak or uninformative gradients. If the generator finds a narrow region that fools the discriminator, mode collapse can occur, where many different latent inputs map to similar outputs. In an interview, it is useful to explain that GAN success depends heavily on balancing the two players, monitoring training dynamics, and stabilizing optimization with architectural or objective-level improvements.

## Why GANs Count as a Bonus Topic
GANs are a useful bonus topic for this assignment because they demonstrate that deep learning is not only about classification or sequence prediction. They also show how different training objectives lead to very different model behavior. In an interview, GANs provide a rich way to discuss tradeoffs between sample quality, stability, and evaluation difficulty. Unlike a classifier with straightforward accuracy metrics, GANs are harder to assess because realism, diversity, and fidelity are not always captured by a single number. A strong answer should connect GANs back to the broader theme of representation and generation. The generator learns a mapping from latent space to data space, while the discriminator acts as a learned training signal. That adversarial pairing is what makes GANs historically influential and technically memorable.
