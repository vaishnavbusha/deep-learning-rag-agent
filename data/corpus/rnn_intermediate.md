# Recurrent Neural Networks

## Sequential State and Temporal Dependence
Recurrent neural networks are built for sequence data, where the meaning of the current element depends on what came before it. Instead of processing each input independently, an RNN maintains a hidden state that is updated at every time step using the current input and the previous hidden state. This gives the model a memory mechanism that, in principle, allows it to encode temporal context across a sequence. A technically solid explanation should mention that the same recurrent weights are reused across time, which is analogous to parameter sharing in CNNs across space. This structure makes RNNs well suited for language, time series, and sequential decision tasks, but it also creates optimization challenges because the model must preserve useful signals across many update steps.

## The Vanishing Gradient Problem
A core limitation of standard recurrent neural networks is the vanishing gradient problem. During backpropagation through time, gradients are repeatedly multiplied as they flow across many sequence steps. If those multipliers are often smaller than one, the gradient shrinks rapidly and early time steps receive almost no meaningful learning signal. As a result, standard RNNs struggle to learn long-range dependencies even though their architecture suggests they should be able to model them. In interviews, this point is often the bridge into discussing LSTMs and gated recurrent units. The important idea is not simply that gradients vanish, but that the training process becomes biased toward short-term patterns because the model cannot reliably assign credit across long temporal spans.

## Why LSTMs Improved on Standard RNNs
Long Short-Term Memory networks were introduced to address the weaknesses of standard RNNs in handling long-range dependencies. They add gating mechanisms that regulate what information is forgotten, what new content is stored, and what is exposed to the next hidden state. This creates a more controlled path for information and gradients to move through time, making training much more stable on tasks where important context may be many steps away. A strong answer should explain that LSTMs are not magic replacements for all sequential models, but they were a major improvement because they provided a practical solution to a very real optimization bottleneck. That is why they became foundational in sequence modeling before attention-based architectures became dominant.
