# Convolutional Neural Networks

## Convolution as Local Feature Extraction
Convolutional neural networks are designed to process grid-structured data such as images by exploiting local spatial structure. Instead of connecting every input pixel to every hidden unit, a convolutional layer applies learnable filters across small receptive fields. Each filter scans the image and produces a feature map that highlights where a learned pattern appears. This parameter sharing makes CNNs far more efficient than fully connected networks for vision tasks because the same detector can be reused across many positions. A good interview answer should stress that convolutions encode an inductive bias: nearby pixels often form meaningful local patterns such as edges, corners, and textures. That bias improves sample efficiency and scalability, especially when image resolution grows.

## Pooling and Translation Robustness
Pooling layers reduce the spatial dimensions of feature maps while preserving the most useful information for downstream layers. Max pooling, for example, keeps the strongest activation within a local neighborhood, which helps the network focus on whether a feature is present rather than its exact position. This contributes to a limited form of translation robustness and also lowers computational cost by shrinking intermediate representations. In practice, pooling is not just a compression trick. It changes the representation by trading exact spatial detail for more stable higher-level features. During an interview, it helps to explain that this is why CNNs often alternate convolution and pooling: convolution detects features, then pooling consolidates them so deeper layers can reason over broader contexts without exploding the number of parameters.

## From Early Layers to Deep Hierarchies
One of the strengths of CNNs is hierarchical representation learning. Early layers often capture simple visual patterns such as oriented edges or color transitions. Middle layers combine these into textures, motifs, or object parts, and deeper layers assemble those parts into task-relevant concepts. This layered hierarchy is why CNNs were so successful in image classification and why architectures such as LeNet and AlexNet became important milestones in deep learning. When discussing CNNs technically, it is useful to mention that deeper networks improve representational power but also increase optimization difficulty. Architectural choices such as kernel size, stride, padding, normalization, and depth influence both accuracy and efficiency. Strong answers connect these choices to the tradeoff between preserving information and building increasingly abstract features.
