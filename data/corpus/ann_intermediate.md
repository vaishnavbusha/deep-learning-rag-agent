# Artificial Neural Networks

## Core Structure of an ANN
Artificial neural networks are layered function approximators inspired by the idea of interconnected neurons, but in practice they are mathematical systems that learn mappings from inputs to outputs through parameterized transformations. A standard feedforward network contains an input layer, one or more hidden layers, and an output layer. Each neuron computes a weighted sum of its inputs, adds a bias term, and applies a nonlinear activation function. The nonlinearity is what allows the network to represent complex decision boundaries rather than collapsing into a single linear transformation. In interview settings, it is important to explain that depth matters because stacked hidden layers allow the model to build progressively richer internal representations. Earlier layers may detect simple patterns, while deeper layers combine those patterns into more abstract features relevant to prediction.

## Backpropagation as the Learning Mechanism
The defining training process for artificial neural networks is backpropagation combined with gradient-based optimization. During the forward pass, the network produces a prediction and a loss function measures how far that prediction is from the target. Backpropagation then applies the chain rule to compute how much each parameter contributed to the error. These gradients are propagated from the output layer back through the hidden layers, allowing the optimizer to update weights in the direction that reduces loss. A strong technical answer should emphasize that backpropagation is not a separate model but the algorithm that makes multilayer networks trainable at scale. It is also useful to note that the quality of learning depends on architecture choice, initialization, activation functions, and optimization settings, because unstable gradients can slow or prevent convergence.

## Why Activation Functions Matter
Activation functions determine how each neuron transforms its weighted input and are central to the expressive power of neural networks. If every layer used only a linear transformation, then multiple layers could be collapsed into one, eliminating the benefit of depth. Nonlinear activations such as sigmoid, tanh, and ReLU allow networks to learn richer mappings. In practice, ReLU became widely used because it reduces some of the saturation problems that affected sigmoid and tanh, helping optimization in deeper models. However, activation choice also affects gradient flow, sparsity, and training stability. In an interview, it is valuable to connect activation functions to learning dynamics rather than treating them as a memorization topic. The right explanation is that activations shape both what a network can represent and how effectively it can be trained through gradient descent.
